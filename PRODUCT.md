# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Curious people mid-rabbit-hole: writers, researchers, students, video/newsletter creators, trivia and history obsessives. They arrive with a fuzzy itch ("forgotten scandals of early internet companies", "scientists mocked for being right") rather than a known page title. Their context is exploratory and open-ended — one tab among many, following a thread, collecting things to read or use later. They are fluent in Wikipedia but frustrated that its discovery is article-first: search a term, land on a page, click blue links by hand.

## Product Purpose

whisk is a discovery layer on top of Wikipedia. Where Wikipedia answers "what is this?", whisk answers "what else is connected to this, and what should I read next?" It turns the encyclopedia into an explorable map: query-first natural-language search that returns clustered results (not a flat list), rabbit-hole paths between topics, an article intelligence panel that surfaces time/place/entities and source-quality signals, and saved collections. Success is the user following a trail they didn't know existed and saving something worth coming back to. whisk never competes with Wikipedia as an encyclopedia — it always links back and preserves attribution.

## Brand Personality

Curious, literate, trustworthy. Three words: **archival, inquisitive, precise.** The voice of a well-read research librarian who finds the strange thread delightful but never sacrifices a citation. Confident and calm, not hypey; playful at the edges ("make it weirder") but the substance is always sourced and honest about uncertainty.

## Anti-references

- Not a chatbot / answer-box. No conversational "Sure! Here's..." UX, no synthesized answer hiding the sources.
- Not generic SaaS: no gradient-mesh hero, no hero-metric template, no identical icon-card grids, no tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows over every section.
- Not the warm-cream "editorial AI" default. Not a Wikipedia clone/reskin.
- Not clickbait "10 CRAZY facts" energy — obscurity is treated with respect, not sensationalism.

## Design Principles

1. **Discovery over answers.** Every surface points outward to more to explore — connections, paths, "more like this" — not a terminal answer.
2. **Source transparency is the moat.** Citations, last-revision, protection status, and links back to Wikipedia are always one glance away. Confidence signals are a feature, not fine print.
3. **Taste and organization are the product.** Wikipedia is free; the value is curation, clustering, and the trail. Ranking blends relevance with *interestingness*.
4. **Calm density.** Show a lot without shouting. Index-card restraint, generous rhythm, one accent for meaning — the tool disappears into the hunt.
5. **AI assists, never impersonates the source.** Generated labels and narration expand and explain; they always cite and never replace article content, and the app is fully functional with AI off.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text ≥4.5:1, large text ≥3:1, including placeholders and text on tinted/colored surfaces. Full keyboard operability for the command search, cluster tabs, cards, and rabbit-hole controls; visible focus rings. Every animation has a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative (crossfade/instant). Color is never the only signal (cluster type, interestingness, and dispute flags carry icons/labels too). Both light and dark themes meet contrast targets.
